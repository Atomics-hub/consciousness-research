# Consciousness Research

## Goal
Understand consciousness deeply enough to work toward preserving it. This is a living research project — part neuroscience, part philosophy, part engineering, part audacity. The same intensity that goes into auditing kernels and finding 0-days goes into this.

Long-term vision: consciousness preservation. Near-term: build the knowledge base to know what's actually possible, what's bullshit, and where the real gaps are.

## Research Domains

### 1. Neuroscience of Consciousness (Foundation)
**Why**: You can't preserve what you don't understand. Need to know what the leading theories actually claim and where they disagree.

**Key theories to study**:
- Integrated Information Theory (IIT) — Tononi. Consciousness = integrated information (Φ). Mathematical framework. Controversial but rigorous
- Global Workspace Theory (GWT) — Baars/Dehaene. Consciousness as broadcast mechanism. Strong empirical support
- Predictive Processing / Free Energy Principle — Friston. Brain as prediction machine. Unifying framework
- Higher-Order Theories — Rosenthal, Lau. Consciousness requires meta-representation
- Recurrent Processing Theory — Lamme. Feedback loops in visual cortex

**Key concepts**:
- Neural Correlates of Consciousness (NCC) — what brain activity maps to conscious experience?
- The binding problem — how does unified experience arise from distributed processing?
- Consciousness vs. attention vs. awareness — these are not the same thing
- Minimal consciousness — what's the simplest system that could be conscious?

**Starter reading**:
- Tononi & Koch, "Consciousness: here, there and everywhere?" (2015)
- Dehaene et al., "A neuronal model of a global workspace in effortful cognitive tasks" (1998)
- Friston, "The free-energy principle: a unified brain theory?" (2010)
- Koch et al., "Neural Correlates of Consciousness: Progress and Problems" (2016)
- Seth, "Being You: A New Science of Consciousness" (2021) — accessible overview

### 2. Philosophy of Mind (The Hard Questions)
**Why**: The engineering won't mean anything if you don't grapple with what consciousness actually *is*. Is a copy of you still you? Does substrate matter?

**Core problems**:
- The Hard Problem (Chalmers) — why is there subjective experience at all?
- Qualia — the redness of red, the painfulness of pain. Are they real? Reducible?
- Personal identity over time — what makes you "you" from moment to moment?
- The teleporter problem — if you're copied perfectly and the original destroyed, did you survive?
- Ship of Theseus for neurons — gradual replacement vs. snapshot-and-rebuild
- Functionalism vs. biological naturalism — does the substrate matter or just the computation?
- Panpsychism — is consciousness fundamental? Integrated Information Theory leans this way
- Philosophical zombies — conceivable? What does that tell us?

**Starter reading**:
- Chalmers, "Facing Up to the Problem of Consciousness" (1995)
- Nagel, "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" (1974)
- Parfit, "Reasons and Persons" (1984) — Part III on personal identity
- Dennett, "Consciousness Explained" (1991) — the counterargument
- Chalmers, "The Conscious Mind" (1996)

### 3. Whole Brain Emulation (WBE) (The Engineering Path)
**Why**: If consciousness is computational, then in principle you can run it on different hardware. This is the most direct path to preservation.

**Key areas**:
- Connectomics — mapping every neuron and synapse. C. elegans (302 neurons) done. Drosophila (139k neurons) done 2024. Human (~86 billion) is the goal
- Scanning technology — electron microscopy, expansion microscopy, light-sheet. What resolution do we actually need?
- Simulation requirements — how much compute for a human brain? Estimates range from 10^18 to 10^25 FLOPS
- The fidelity question — do you need molecular-level simulation or is neuron-level enough?
- Sandberg & Bostrom roadmap — the most serious technical analysis of WBE feasibility

**Starter reading**:
- Sandberg & Bostrom, "Whole Brain Emulation: A Roadmap" (2008) — the bible
- Lichtman & Denk, "The Big and the Small: Challenges of Imaging the Brain's Circuits" (2011)
- Markram et al., "Reconstruction and Simulation of Neocortical Microcircuitry" (2015) — Blue Brain Project
- Shapson-Coe et al., "A petavoxel fragment of human cerebral cortex" (2024) — Google/Harvard 1mm³ map

### 4. Brain-Computer Interfaces (The Bridge)
**Why**: BCIs are the near-term technology that bridges biology and silicon. Read first, write later, merge eventually.

**Current state**:
- Neuralink N1 — 1024 electrodes, first human implant 2024. Motor cortex focus
- Utah arrays — gold standard for decades, ~100 electrodes. Degrades over years
- Stentrode — endovascular, no open-brain surgery. Motor neuroprosthesis
- Neuropixels — 5000+ recording sites, research tool
- ECoG grids — surface-level, less invasive, lower resolution

**Key questions**:
- Read bandwidth: how many neurons can we simultaneously record? Currently ~tens of thousands
- Write bandwidth: can we meaningfully stimulate? Crude compared to reading
- Longevity: how long do implants last in vivo?
- What would "uploading" via BCI even look like? Gradual migration?

**Starter reading**:
- Musk & Neuralink, "An Integrated Brain-Machine Interface Platform" (2019)
- Lebedev & Nicolelis, "Brain-Machine Interfaces: From Basic Science to Neuroprostheses and Neurorehabilitation" (2017)
- Oxley et al., "Motor neuroprosthesis implanted with neurointerventional surgery" (2021) — Stentrode

### 5. Cryonics & Preservation (The Backup Plan)
**Why**: If we can't emulate in our lifetime, can we at least preserve the information for someone who can?

**Key approaches**:
- Vitrification — flash-cooling with cryoprotectants to prevent ice crystal damage. Alcor, Cryonics Institute
- Aldehyde-Stabilized Cryopreservation (ASC) — chemical fixation + vitrification. Won Brain Preservation Prize 2018
- Brain Banking — research tissue preservation. Not aimed at revival but techniques overlap
- Plastination — chemical embedding. Preserves structure permanently but... you're plastic

**Key questions**:
- Does vitrification actually preserve connectome? Evidence says mostly yes for ASC
- What information is lost? Molecular states? Synaptic weights? Neurotransmitter levels?
- Is structural preservation enough, or do you need dynamic state?
- Legal, ethical, logistical realities of cryonics

**Starter reading**:
- McIntyre & Bhatt, "Aldehyde-Stabilized Cryopreservation" (2015) — Brain Preservation Prize winner
- Merkle, "The Technical Feasibility of Cryonics" (1992)
- de Wolf et al., "Cryopreservation and Revival: A Comprehensive Review" (2020)
- Phaedrus, "Wait But Why: Why Cryonics Makes Sense" — not a paper but damn good explainer

### 6. AI & Consciousness (The Mirror)
**Why**: You work with AI daily. The question of whether AI can be conscious isn't academic — it's becoming urgent. And computational theories of mind blur the line.

**Key questions**:
- Can LLMs be conscious? Almost certainly not with current architectures, but why exactly?
- What would a conscious AI architecture need? Recurrence? Embodiment? Self-model?
- If IIT is right, Φ should be measurable — has anyone tried on neural networks?
- The alignment problem intersects here: a conscious AI has moral status
- Could we build consciousness "from scratch" before we can emulate biological consciousness?

**Starter reading**:
- Butlin et al., "Consciousness in Artificial Intelligence: Insights from the Science of Consciousness" (2023) — major multi-author report
- Schneider & Turner, "Is Anyone Home? A Way to Find Out If AI Has Become Self-Aware" (2017)
- Tononi & Koch, "Consciousness: here, there and everywhere?" (2015) — IIT implications for AI
- Schwitzgebel & Garza, "A Defense of the Rights of Artificial Intelligences" (2015)

### 7. Information-Theoretic & Physics-Based Approaches
**Why**: Some theories ground consciousness in physics itself. Wild but worth understanding.

**Theories**:
- IIT mathematical framework — Φ as a measure, exclusion postulate, cause-effect structures
- Orchestrated Objective Reduction (Orch OR) — Penrose & Hameroff. Quantum coherence in microtubules. Controversial, mostly dismissed, but not definitively disproven
- Quantum mind theories generally — decoherence times in warm wet brains make this hard
- Constructor theory — Deutsch & Marletto. Information as fundamental. Potential consciousness implications

**Starter reading**:
- Tononi et al., "Integrated Information Theory: An Updated Account" (2016)
- Hameroff & Penrose, "Consciousness in the universe: A review of the 'Orch OR' theory" (2014)
- Tegmark, "Consciousness as a State of Matter" (2015)

## Open Questions (The Ones That Keep You Up)
1. Is consciousness substrate-independent? (If no, emulation is fundamentally limited)
2. What is the minimum sufficient description of a brain state to preserve "you"?
3. Is gradual neuron replacement (ship of Theseus) more philosophically sound than snapshot-and-copy?
4. Can consciousness be formally defined in a way that's testable?
5. What's the actual timeline for connectome-level scanning of a human brain?
6. If you perfectly simulate a brain, is the simulation conscious or just behaving as if it is?
7. Does continuity of consciousness matter, or is identity just pattern?
