## 1. COGITATE Adversarial Collaboration (Nature, 2025)

**Paper:** "Adversarial testing of global neuronal workspace and integrated information theories of consciousness"
**Authors:** The COGITATE Consortium (led by Lucia Melloni)
**Published:** Nature, 2025

### Core Finding

The first large-scale, preregistered adversarial collaboration directly testing two leading theories of consciousness against each other. Both theories took hits. Neither was decisively confirmed or refuted.

### Methodology

- **Participants:** 256 humans across seven independent labs worldwide
- **Neuroimaging:** Three modalities -- fMRI (n=73), MEG (n=65), intracranial EEG (n=29-31)
- **Stimuli:** Suprathreshold visual stimuli varying in four dimensions: category (faces, objects, letters, false fonts), identity, orientation, and duration (0.5s, 1.0s, 1.5s)
- **Task design:** Stimuli were either task-relevant or task-irrelevant
- **Preregistration:** All hypotheses, analyses, and interpretations locked in advance. Theory proponents committed to accepting results regardless of outcome.

Three specific predictions were tested, each with divergent expectations from IIT and GNWT.

### Prediction 1: Where is conscious content decoded?

- **IIT predicted:** Maximum decoding in posterior cortex (the "posterior hot zone")
- **GNWT predicted:** Critical involvement of prefrontal cortex (PFC)
- **Result:** Information about conscious content was found in visual, ventrotemporal, and inferior frontal cortex. Decoding was strongest in posterior regions, consistent with IIT. PFC showed limited representation of certain conscious dimensions, partially challenging GNWT.
- **Scorecard:** IIT supported. GNWT partially challenged.

### Prediction 2: How is conscious content maintained over time?

- **IIT predicted:** Sustained neural responses throughout stimulus duration
- **GNWT predicted:** Brief "ignition" events at stimulus onset and offset (~0.3-0.5s)
- **Result:** Sustained responses were found in occipital and lateral temporal cortex reflecting stimulus duration. The predicted ignition at stimulus offset was generally absent.
- **Scorecard:** IIT supported. GNWT challenged on the ignition prediction.

### Prediction 3: How do brain regions communicate during consciousness?

- **IIT predicted:** Sustained synchronization within the posterior cortex network
- **GNWT predicted:** Content-specific frontal-posterior synchronization
- **Result:** Content-specific synchronization between frontal and early visual areas was observed, but sustained posterior synchronization was absent. IIT proponents counter that sparse electrode coverage in iEEG may explain the null finding.
- **Scorecard:** IIT challenged. GNWT partially supported.

### Net Result

IIT: 2 of 3 predictions confirmed, 1 failed.
GNWT: 0 of 3 predictions clearly confirmed (partial support on prediction 3, challenged on predictions 1 and 2).

### Strengths

- **Methodological gold standard.** Preregistered, adversarial, multi-site, multi-modality, theory-neutral consortium. This is how science should work.
- **Scale.** 256 participants across three neuroimaging techniques gives real statistical power.
- **Transparency.** Full datasets and analysis pipelines released publicly.
- **Intellectual honesty.** Both teams agreed to accept results in advance. No post-hoc rationalization baked into the design.

### Weaknesses and Criticisms

- **Suprathreshold stimuli only.** Testing consciousness with clearly visible stimuli doesn't probe the boundary conditions (threshold, subliminal) where theories diverge most sharply.
- **Visual perception only.** Consciousness involves hunger, pain, emotion, proprioception, inner speech -- none tested. The posterior hot zone thesis may not generalize beyond vision.
- **IIT's prediction 3 escape hatch.** The failed synchronization prediction is attributed by IIT proponents to sparse electrode coverage. This is plausible but unfalsifiable -- you can always blame the measurement.
- **GNWT's ignition is hard to pin down.** What exactly counts as "ignition"? The prediction was somewhat vague, making failure ambiguous.
- **Only two theories tested.** Higher-order theories, recurrent processing theory, predictive processing, and attention schema theory were not included.

### Implications for Consciousness Preservation

The results suggest that consciousness is associated with sustained activity in posterior cortex reflecting content and duration. If consciousness depends on continuous, time-locked neural activity patterns rather than discrete broadcast events, this matters for preservation: you need to preserve the substrate's capacity for sustained, structured activity, not just connectivity or static structure.

### Open Questions

- Would the results hold for non-visual modalities (pain, emotion, audition)?
- What happens at perceptual thresholds where conscious/unconscious diverge?
- Is the posterior hot zone specific to visual consciousness or a general substrate?
- Can either theory be salvaged by revision, or are they fundamentally wrong?

## 2. IIT 4.0 (Tononi et al., 2023)

**Paper:** "Integrated information theory (IIT) 4.0: Formulating the properties of phenomenal existence in physical terms"
**Authors:** Albantakis, Barbosa, Findlay, Grasso, Haun, Marshall, Mayner, Zaeemzadeh, Boly, Juel, Sasai, Fujii, David, Hendren, Lang, & Tononi
**Published:** PLOS Computational Biology, October 2023

### Core Argument

IIT 4.0 is the latest and most formally rigorous version of the theory. It starts from properties of experience (axioms), derives requirements for the physical substrate (postulates), and expresses those requirements mathematically. The fundamental claim: consciousness IS integrated information. Not "correlates with" -- IS.

### The Architecture: Axioms to Postulates

**Zeroth Axiom:** Experience exists. ("There is something.") This is treated as self-evident and irrefutable.

**Five Axioms (properties of experience):**
1. **Intrinsicality** -- Experience exists for itself, not for an external observer
2. **Information** -- Experience is specific (this experience, not another)
3. **Integration** -- Experience is unitary, irreducible to separate experiences
4. **Exclusion** -- Experience is definite (bounded, this whole and nothing more)
5. **Composition** -- Experience is structured (composed of distinctions and relations)

**Five Postulates (requirements on the physical substrate):**
Each axiom maps to a postulate constraining what physical systems can be conscious:
1. **Intrinsicality** -- Cause-effect power must be intrinsic (within the system)
2. **Information** -- Cause-effect power must be specific (this state, this cause-effect state)
3. **Integration** -- System must specify its cause-effect state as a whole, irreducible to partitions
4. **Exclusion** -- One and only one substrate (the "complex") with maximum integrated information
5. **Composition** -- Subsets specify cause-effect states forming distinctions and relations (the "Phi-structure")

### Mathematical Machinery

- **Intrinsic information (ii):** Product of informativeness and selectivity. Tension between expansion (more units = more deviation from chance) and dilution (more units = less concentrated effect).
- **Integrated information (phi):** Minimum of cause integrated information (phi_c) and effect integrated information (phi_e). Measures information loss when partitioning the system.
- **System integrated information (phi_s):** phi over the system's minimum partition.
- **Phi-structure:** The complete cause-effect structure unfolded from the maximal substrate, comprising distinctions and relations.
- **Structure integrated information (Phi):** Sum of phi values across all distinctions and relations. This is the quantity of consciousness.

### Key Changes from IIT 3.0

1. **New intrinsic information measure.** IIT 3.0 used Earth Mover's Distance; IIT 4.0 introduces "intrinsic difference" (ID) designed to satisfy the postulates uniquely.
2. **Explicit causal relations.** Formal treatment of how distinctions relate to each other, not just their individual phi values.
3. **Complete Phi-structure characterization.** First version claiming to characterize the full structure of experience, not just its quantity.
4. **More rigorous axiom-to-postulate mapping.** Tighter logical chain from phenomenology to physics.

### Strengths

- **Internally consistent formal framework.** Whatever you think of the axioms, the mathematical derivation from them is rigorous. It's a real theory, not hand-waving.
- **Makes specific predictions.** Consciousness requires posterior cortex, not cerebellum (feed-forward). Consciousness fades in dreamless sleep because integration decreases. These are testable.
- **Takes the hard problem seriously.** Rather than deflecting ("consciousness is just information processing"), IIT tries to identify what consciousness actually IS in physical terms.
- **The "explanatory identity" is bold.** Every property of experience maps to a property of the Phi-structure. This is the strongest possible claim a theory can make.

### Weaknesses and Criticisms

- **Computational intractability.** Computing Phi scales super-exponentially. You cannot compute it for any realistic neural system. This means the theory's core quantity is practically unmeasurable for brains. Proxy measures (Phi*, Phi_G) have no proven relationship to actual Phi and can give qualitatively different results.
- **The axioms aren't really axioms.** Tim Bayne argues they fail to qualify as genuine axioms (self-evident truths). They're more like assumptions or stipulations about what consciousness is like, and reasonable people can disagree with them.
- **The Aaronson problem.** Scott Aaronson showed that under IIT's own formalism, an inactive grid of logic gates arranged correctly would be more conscious than a human brain. Tononi accepts this as a genuine prediction rather than a reductio ad absurdum. Whether you find this concerning is a litmus test for your tolerance of counterintuitive conclusions.
- **Panpsychism as feature, not bug?** IIT implies consciousness is widespread -- thermostats, logic gates, potentially plants. IIT proponents embrace this. Critics see it as a reductio.
- **The unfolding argument is weak.** IIT's main argument against computational functionalism (the "unfolding argument") has been criticized as not actually establishing that integrated information IS consciousness rather than merely correlating with it.
- **Proxy measures problem is severe.** If you can never compute the actual quantity that defines consciousness, you can never actually test the theory at its core. You're always testing approximations of unknown fidelity. This is like having a security proof that's computationally infeasible to verify.

### Implications for Consciousness Preservation

IIT 4.0 implies that what matters for consciousness is the cause-effect structure (Phi-structure) of the physical substrate. Preserving consciousness would require preserving the substrate's capacity to realize the same Phi-structure -- same causal architecture, same integration. A digital upload that preserves functional organization but not intrinsic cause-effect power would NOT be conscious under IIT, because digital systems are modular/feed-forward at the gate level and lack intrinsic integration. This is the most pessimistic major theory for digital consciousness preservation.

### Open Questions

- Can Phi ever be computed for realistic systems, or is the theory permanently untestable at scale?
- Are the proxy measures reliable enough to serve as stand-ins?
- Is the Aaronson counterexample a genuine prediction or a bug in the formalism?
- Does the posterior hot zone prediction hold beyond visual consciousness?
- How does IIT 4.0 handle split-brain cases, dissociative identity, or gradual neural replacement?

## 3. Butlin et al. (2023) -- "Consciousness in Artificial Intelligence: Insights from the Science of Consciousness"

**Paper:** Originally arXiv preprint (August 2023), published in Trends in Cognitive Sciences (2025) as "Identifying indicators of consciousness in AI systems"
**Authors:** Patrick Butlin, Robert Long, Tim Bayne, Yoshua Bengio, David Chalmers, and 14 others

### Core Argument

Rather than picking one theory of consciousness, use ALL the leading scientific theories to derive "indicator properties" -- computational features that, if present, would raise the probability that a system is conscious. Then assess current AI systems against these indicators.

### The Theory-Heavy Approach

The authors explicitly argue against two approaches:
1. **Behavior-based assessment** -- unreliable because systems can behave as-if conscious without being conscious (and vice versa)
2. **Single-theory dependence** -- too uncertain, given we don't know which theory is correct

Instead, they advocate a **theory-heavy, multi-theory** approach: draw indicators from every well-supported theory and treat them as probabilistic evidence. More indicators satisfied = higher credence in consciousness.

### Indicator Properties by Theory

**From Recurrent Processing Theory (RPT):**
- Algorithmic recurrence (feedback loops between perceptual and higher-order areas)
- Organized, integrated perceptual representations generated by input modules

**From Global Workspace Theory (GWT):**
- Multiple specialized processing modules
- A workspace mechanism for integrating and broadcasting information
- State-dependent attention determining what enters the workspace
- Global broadcast back to all modules

**From Higher-Order Theories (HOT):**
- Metacognitive monitoring mechanism
- Higher-order representations of first-order perceptual states
- These higher-order states determine which perceptual representations contribute to belief formation

**From Predictive Processing:**
- Hierarchical processing with top-down predictions and bottom-up error signals
- Active inference / prediction error minimization
- Generative model of the world

**From Attention Schema Theory (AST):**
- Attention mechanisms
- An internal model representing the system's own state of attention
- Self-monitoring of attentional allocation

### Assessment of Current AI

**Conclusion:** No current AI system satisfies enough indicators to warrant attributing consciousness. However, there are no obvious *technical* barriers to building systems that would satisfy them.

**On LLMs specifically:** LLMs like GPT-4 are primarily feed-forward during inference (no recurrence), lack persistent workspace mechanisms, don't have genuine attention schemas (despite using "attention" mechanisms), and don't perform active inference. The "attention" in transformers is a mathematical operation, not the kind of selective, capacity-limited attention that consciousness theories describe.

### Strengths

- **Epistemically humble.** Doesn't bet on one theory. Acknowledges deep uncertainty.
- **Actionable framework.** Gives engineers concrete features to look for or avoid.
- **Star-studded author list** -- Chalmers, Bengio, Bayne, Schwitzgebel, etc. This isn't fringe work.
- **Separates the question properly.** Behavioral evidence is unreliable; theory-driven assessment is better. This is correct.

### Weaknesses and Criticisms

- **The indicators are vague.** "Organized, integrated perceptual representations" could describe a well-designed database. "Metacognitive monitoring" could describe any system that logs its own state. The computational descriptions are loose enough that creative interpretation could find them almost anywhere -- or nowhere.
- **Circular risk.** The theories were developed to explain biological consciousness. Deriving indicators from them and then asking "does this AI have them?" may just be asking "does this AI look like a brain?" -- which begs the question of whether brain-likeness is necessary.
- **No weighting scheme.** All indicators are presented without relative importance. Satisfying 3 of 5 GWT indicators and 0 HOT indicators gives no clear verdict. The framework doesn't tell you how to aggregate.
- **Conflicting theories, conflicting indicators.** IIT says feed-forward systems can't be conscious. GWT says broadcast is the key. If a system satisfies GWT indicators but not IIT's, what do you conclude? The multi-theory approach creates an incoherent mess when theories disagree at the fundamental level.
- **The "no technical barriers" claim is too strong.** Saying there are no obvious barriers to building conscious AI is a substantive empirical claim that the paper doesn't justify beyond noting that the indicators are computationally describable.
- **Missing the hard problem.** The entire framework assumes that implementing the right functional organization IS consciousness (functionalism). If consciousness depends on something beyond function (as IIT 4.0, biological computationalism, and others argue), then satisfying all the indicators might still produce a zombie.

### Implications for Consciousness Preservation

The Butlin framework suggests that consciousness is multiply realizable -- if you satisfy the indicators in any substrate, you might be conscious. This is the most optimistic position for digital preservation/uploading. But the framework's vagueness means it can't tell you with confidence whether any particular implementation actually works.

### Open Questions

- How should indicators be weighted against each other?
- What happens when theories give contradictory verdicts on the same system?
- Can functional indicators ever be sufficient, or is something non-functional needed?
- How do you distinguish genuine satisfaction of an indicator from superficial mimicry?
- Should the framework be updated as theories are empirically tested (e.g., COGITATE results)?

## 4. Vitrification and Functional Recovery (PNAS, 2026)

**Paper:** "Functional recovery of the adult murine hippocampus after cryopreservation by vitrification"
**Authors:** Researchers from Friedrich-Alexander University Erlangen-Nuremberg
**Published:** PNAS, March 2026 (submitted June 2025, accepted January 2026)

### Core Finding

Adult mouse hippocampal brain slices were vitrified (cooled into a glass-like state), stored at cryogenic temperatures for up to 7 days, rewarmed, and showed near-physiological electrophysiological recovery -- including action potentials, synaptic transmission, and long-term potentiation (LTP). This is the strongest demonstration yet that functional neural activity can survive cryopreservation.

### Methodology

**Vitrification solution (V3):**
- 22.3% w/v dimethyl sulfoxide
- 16.84% w/v ethylene glycol
- 12.86% w/v formamide
- 7% w/v polyvinylpyrrolidone K12
- Peak concentration: 59% w/v V3 (8.42 M permeable cryoprotectant)

**Cooling protocol:**
- Stepwise CPA loading at controlled temperatures (0C to -10C)
- Rapid cooling at ~130C/s via directional cooling on liquid nitrogen-cooled copper cylinder
- One minute on cylinder, then transfer to liquid nitrogen

**Storage:** -150C (slices) or -140C (whole brain) for 10 minutes to 7 days

**Warming protocol:**
- ~80C/s warming rate
- Rewarming into 52% V3 at -10C
- Stepwise CPA unloading: 45%, 30%, 16%, 8%, 4%, 2%, 0% V3

### Electrophysiology Results (Slice Vitrification)

**Resting membrane potential:**
- CA1 pyramidal cells: -74.82 +/- 1.12 mV (vitrified) vs -74.66 +/- 0.66 mV (control). P = 0.956. **Essentially identical.**
- DG granule cells: -83.17 +/- 1.43 mV (vitrified) vs -85.97 +/- 0.53 mV (control). P = 0.037. **Small but significant depolarization.**

**Input resistance and capacitance:**
- CA1: No significant changes
- DG: Reduced membrane capacitance (58.18 vs 75.65 pF, P = 0.001), suggesting some membrane surface area loss

**Synaptic transmission (Schaffer collateral -> CA1):**
- Reduced at maximum stimulus intensities (downward deflection in input-output curve)
- Short-term plasticity: Attenuated compared to controls

**Long-term potentiation (LTP):**
- SC-CA1: 138.06 +/- 6.90% (vitrified) vs 157.68 +/- 7.14% (control). P = 0.072. **Not significantly different.**
- mPP-DG: 147.96 +/- 7.20% (vitrified) vs 125.04 +/- 5.03% (control). P = 0.021. **Enhanced in vitrified tissue.**

**Action potentials:**
- CA1: Increased rheobase (higher current needed to fire), reduced firing during ramp depolarization, increased afterhyperpolarization
- DG: Comparable rheobase, enhanced excitability at larger depolarizations, faster/narrower APs

**Spontaneous activity:** No significant difference from controls
**E/I balance:** 2.14 vs 1.80 (P = 0.255) -- preserved

### Whole-Brain Results

This is where it gets ugly:
- **Gradual CPA loading:** Severe cerebral dehydration (44.5% of reference mass). Darkening. No recoverable synaptic responses. Failure.
- **Shock-loading with full-strength V3:** Better mass retention (55.7%) but still problematic.
- **Interleaved equilibration:** 69.8% mass retention, viable tissue obtained.
- **Success rate:** 1 of 3 iterations produced viable tissue. Others excluded due to inadequate perfusion or absent electrophysiology.
- **Short-term plasticity from whole-brain vitrification:** "Markedly reduced" compared to slice vitrification.

### Metabolic Data

CPA toxicity is dose-dependent:
- Control basal respiration: 173.3 pmol/min
- 59% V3: 135.1 pmol/min
- 65% V3: 80.4 pmol/min
- Spearman's r = -0.9018, p = 0.0002

Critically, vitrification itself did not add damage beyond CPA exposure alone -- the toxicity is from the cryoprotectant, not the freezing.

### Structural Preservation

- Electron microscopy: Well-preserved ultrastructure
- Transient mitochondrial swelling post-rewarming, resolving within 1-10 hours
- Dendritic spine density and length: Comparable to controls
- Nissl staining: Normal morphology

### Strengths

- **Quantitative rigor.** Patch-clamp recordings with proper controls, statistical comparisons, multiple cell types tested. This is real physiology, not hand-waving.
- **LTP preservation is the headline result.** LTP is the closest thing we have to a cellular correlate of learning/memory. The fact that it's preserved (and even enhanced in DG) is remarkable.
- **RMP preservation is striking.** The resting membrane potential -- the most fundamental electrophysiological property -- was statistically identical in CA1 cells. This means the basic ionic machinery survived intact.
- **Toxicity vs freezing dissociation.** Showing that damage comes from CPA, not vitrification itself, means engineering better CPAs could dramatically improve results.

### Weaknesses and Limitations

- **Slice vitrification is not whole-brain vitrification.** The slice results are impressive. The whole-brain results are a mess -- 1/3 success rate, severe dehydration, degraded plasticity. The gap between slices and intact brains is enormous.
- **Short observation window.** Acute slices naturally deteriorate after 10-15 hours. We don't know if functional recovery persists long-term.
- **Not translatable to clinical scenarios.** The authors explicitly state: results "should not be interpreted as directly translatable to cryopreservation of large organs." The model "does not reflect perimortal alterations." This is not about bringing back cryopreserved humans.
- **Limited cell types tested.** Only CA1 pyramidal cells, DG granule cells, and CA1 interneurons. What about cortical neurons, inhibitory interneuron subtypes, glia, vasculature?
- **Some functions degraded.** Short-term plasticity is attenuated. CA1 firing properties changed (higher threshold, different AHP). These aren't trivial -- STP is critical for temporal coding, and altered excitability could mean circuit-level dysfunction.
- **Scaling problem.** Cooling/warming rates of 80-130C/s are achievable for small slices but physically impossible for human brains due to thermal conductivity limits.

### Implications for Consciousness Preservation

This is the most directly relevant paper to the question of whether cryopreservation can preserve the substrate of consciousness. The results show that the basic building blocks -- membrane potentials, synaptic transmission, and LTP -- can survive vitrification at the slice level. However, consciousness presumably requires intact, large-scale circuit function, not just individual synapses firing. The whole-brain results show we're nowhere near that. The finding that CPA toxicity (not freezing) is the main damage source is encouraging -- it's an engineering problem, not a fundamental physical barrier.

### Open Questions

- Can CPA toxicity be reduced enough for whole-brain vitrification without compromise?
- Do circuit-level computations (oscillations, ensemble coding, network dynamics) survive?
- What about white matter, long-range connectivity, neuromodulatory systems?
- Is 7 days the limit, or would years/decades of storage produce additional damage?
- Can this be scaled to primate or human brains?

## 5. The 124-Scholar Letter Against IIT (PsyArXiv, 2023)

**Paper:** "The Integrated Information Theory of Consciousness as Pseudoscience"
**Authors:** Hakwan Lau, and 123 co-signatories including Bernard Baars, Daniel Dennett, Joseph LeDoux
**Published:** PsyArXiv preprint, September 15, 2023

### Core Argument

IIT should be labeled pseudoscience because its core claims are not empirically testable, it masquerades as established science in popular media, and its panpsychist implications are scientifically unsupported.

### Specific Objections

**1. Untestable core claims**
The letter argues that while IIT makes some testable peripheral predictions (like the posterior hot zone), its central claim -- that consciousness IS integrated information (Phi) -- cannot be empirically tested. You would need independent access to consciousness (which we don't have) to verify that Phi tracks it.

**2. Panpsychism as reductio**
IIT implies that inactive grids of logic gates, thermostats, and possibly plants are conscious. The letter treats this as a reductio ad absurdum rather than a genuine prediction. If a theory says an unplugged grid of AND gates is more conscious than a human, something is wrong with the theory.

**3. Media misrepresentation**
IIT has been described in popular outlets as "the only really promising fundamental theory of consciousness." Proponents have made strong claims about thermostat consciousness in public forums. The letter argues this overstates the theory's empirical support.

**4. Mathematical problems (via Hanson)**
Jake Hanson's work demonstrates that Phi is not mathematically well-defined in important cases, and that the theory's predictions break down in simple test cases (conscious vs unconscious toll booths). The formalism doesn't reliably distinguish systems we'd consider conscious from those we wouldn't.

**5. Biological incompatibility**
IIT ignores facts about neural degeneracy (many different neural configurations producing the same function) and redundancy. It's also criticized for focusing on the posterior hot zone while ignoring that hunger, pain, olfaction, and other qualia involve regions outside this zone.

**6. The unfolding argument fails**
IIT's main argument against computational functionalism (that simulations lack intrinsic cause-effect power) is criticized as not establishing that integrated information IS consciousness, only that it correlates with certain properties.

### Notable Signatories and Their Positions

- **Bernard Baars** (GWT originator) -- direct competitor theory
- **Daniel Dennett** -- rejects phenomenal consciousness as traditionally conceived
- **Joseph LeDoux** -- skeptic of consciousness science generally
- **Hakwan Lau** (lead author) -- higher-order theory proponent, direct competitor

### Responses from IIT Proponents and Others

- **Anil Seth** (not an IIT proponent): Called the pseudoscience label "inflammatory." Argued IIT's core claims are harder to test because the theory is more ambitious, but that doesn't make it pseudoscience.
- **Christof Koch:** IIT "makes its assumptions very clear," which is what science should do.
- **Erik Hoel:** The same criticisms (untestable core, counterintuitive implications) apply to GWT and other theories. Why single out IIT?
- **Liad Mudrik:** Researchers "managed to falsify one of its predictions" in COGITATE, proving IIT IS testable.

### Are the Objections Valid?

**The strong objections:**
- **Computational intractability is real.** You genuinely cannot compute Phi for realistic systems. This is not a minor implementation detail -- it means the theory's core quantity is practically unmeasurable. This is a serious structural problem.
- **The Aaronson problem is real.** Logic gates being more conscious than brains is a genuine prediction of IIT, not a misunderstanding. Whether you accept it as a reductio depends on your priors, but it should give anyone pause.
- **Hanson's mathematical critiques are real.** If Phi isn't well-defined in simple cases, the formalism needs fixing.

**The weak objections:**
- **"Pseudoscience" is the wrong label.** IIT is unfalsifiable at its core, but so is every fundamental theory of consciousness (including GWT -- how do you test that broadcast IS consciousness rather than correlating with it?). Calling IIT pseudoscience while giving GWT a pass is inconsistent. The Popperian criterion is too blunt for foundational theories.
- **Panpsychism is not a reductio by itself.** Panpsychism is a respectable philosophical position with a long history. Dismissing IIT because it implies panpsychism is a philosophical objection, not a scientific one. The letter conflates "I find this conclusion implausible" with "this is pseudoscience."
- **Competitor bias is evident.** The letter is led by higher-order theory proponents and signed by GWT's originator. The signatories are largely people whose theories are in direct competition with IIT. This doesn't invalidate their arguments, but it's a conflict of interest that should be noted.

**The mixed objections:**
- **Media overrepresentation.** This is partly valid (IIT has gotten outsized media coverage relative to its empirical support), but it's not the theory's fault that journalists prefer dramatic claims. And other theories get uncritical coverage too.

### Implications for Consciousness Preservation

The letter's strongest argument against IIT -- that you can't compute Phi -- has a practical implication: even if IIT is correct, you can't use it to assess whether a preservation method maintains consciousness because you can't measure the relevant quantity. You'd need proxy measures, and those proxies may be unreliable.

### Open Questions

- Is the "pseudoscience" label constructive or just polarizing?
- Can IIT's mathematical formalism be fixed to address Hanson's critiques?
- Does the COGITATE result (2/3 IIT predictions confirmed) undercut the "untestable" charge?
- Should the same standards applied to IIT be applied to all consciousness theories?

## 6. Biological Computationalism (Aru & Milinkovic, 2025)

**Paper:** "On biological and artificial consciousness: A case for biological computationalism"
**Authors:** Borjan Milinkovic & Jaan Aru
**Published:** Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews, December 2025

### Core Argument

There's a third position between computational functionalism ("the right computation in any substrate = consciousness") and biological naturalism ("only biological brains can be conscious"). Biological computationalism argues that consciousness requires a specific *type* of computation -- hybrid, scale-inseparable, metabolically grounded -- that biological systems perform but current digital systems do not.

### The Three Defining Properties

**1. Hybrid continuous-discrete computation**
Brains don't do purely digital or purely analog computation. Neurons spike (discrete events) while embedded in continuous fields of voltage, chemical gradients, ionic diffusion, and time-varying conductances. The continuous dynamics aren't noise or implementation detail -- they're part of the computation. Digital systems process information through discrete steps only. Even "analog" AI accelerators are still approximating continuous dynamics in fundamentally discrete ways.

**2. Scale-inseparable multiscale processing**
In conventional computers, there's a clean boundary between hardware and software, between transistor physics and program logic. In brains, no such boundary exists. Ion channel states affect dendritic integration, which affects circuit dynamics, which affects whole-brain states, which feed back to modulate ion channels. The causal mechanisms "run through multiple scales at once." This isn't accidental complexity -- it's how the brain achieves computational power within metabolic constraints. Changing the substrate changes the computation because the computation IS the substrate dynamics.

**3. Metabolic grounding**
Energy constraints don't just limit the brain -- they shape what it can represent, how it learns, which dynamics are stable, and how information flows. The tight coupling between energy metabolism and computation is not an unfortunate constraint but an optimization strategy. Scale inseparability emerged BECAUSE of metabolic pressure -- reusing computational work across scales is energetically efficient.

### The Argument Against Substrate Independence

The standard functionalist argument: if you implement the same input-output function in silicon, you get the same consciousness. Aru and Milinkovic's counter: you CAN'T implement the same function because the function isn't separable from the substrate. "In biological computation, the algorithm IS the substrate." The continuous fields, ion flows, dendritic integration, oscillatory coupling, and electromagnetic interactions aren't peripheral details you can abstract away -- they ARE the computational primitives.

This is analogous to arguing that you can't separate a program from its hardware when the hardware's analog physics ARE the program. A weather simulation computes the same function as the weather, but it's not wet. Similarly, a neural network simulation computes an approximation of brain function, but it doesn't have the substrate-dependent properties that (allegedly) give rise to consciousness.

### What They Say About Non-Biological Consciousness

Crucially, they do NOT argue that only carbon biology can be conscious. They argue that only systems with hybrid, scale-inseparable, metabolically grounded computation can be conscious. If you built a non-biological substrate that genuinely instantiated these properties -- not simulated them, but physically performed them -- it could in principle be conscious. The substrate material matters less than the computational organization type.

This is a subtle position: it rules out digital computers running neural network simulations (because those are discrete, scale-separable, and not metabolically grounded), but it doesn't rule out hypothetical engineered substrates that operate like biological tissue.

### Strengths

- **Addresses a real gap.** Most consciousness theories either say "any substrate works" (functionalism) or "only brains work" (biological naturalism). Neither position is well-justified. Biological computationalism occupies genuinely new territory.
- **Grounded in real neuroscience.** The claims about hybrid computation, scale-inseparability, and metabolic constraints are empirically supported. Brains really do work this way. The question is whether it MATTERS for consciousness.
- **Non-trivially falsifiable.** If someone builds a digital system that is clearly conscious, biological computationalism is falsified. If biological systems can be shown to be conscious purely in virtue of their abstract computational structure (ignoring substrate details), biological computationalism is falsified.
- **Avoids the extremes.** It doesn't make the strong claim that only neurons can be conscious (which would be unjustified biological chauvinism). It identifies specific computational properties as necessary, which is a more defensible position.

### Weaknesses and Criticisms

- **The core claim is unproven.** That scale-inseparability and hybrid computation are NECESSARY for consciousness (not just features of how brains happen to do it) is an assertion, not a demonstrated fact. Maybe consciousness only needs the abstract computational pattern, and the substrate-specific details are just implementation.
- **No formal framework.** Unlike IIT, there's no mathematical formalism. "Scale-inseparable multiscale processing" is descriptive, not quantitative. How much scale inseparability is enough? What's the threshold for "hybrid enough"? Without formalization, the theory is hard to test.
- **"The algorithm is the substrate" is slippery.** This could mean something profound (computation is literally constituted by substrate physics) or something trivial (all computation is physical). The paper doesn't clearly distinguish these readings.
- **The weather analogy cuts both ways.** Yes, a weather simulation isn't wet. But a flight simulator accurately predicts flight dynamics without being a plane. Function can be substrate-independent even when the original implementation is substrate-dependent. The paper doesn't adequately address this.
- **No experimental predictions.** The paper is purely theoretical. It doesn't propose experiments that would distinguish biological computationalism from functionalism or biological naturalism.
- **Potentially unfalsifiable in practice.** If a digital system behaves exactly like a conscious being, biological computationalists can always say "but it's not REALLY conscious because it lacks scale-inseparability." This is the same unfalsifiability problem that plagues all consciousness theories.

### Implications for Consciousness Preservation

Biological computationalism is moderately pessimistic for digital preservation. A standard mind upload (scanning the brain and simulating it on digital hardware) would NOT preserve consciousness because it loses the hybrid, scale-inseparable, metabolically grounded computation. However, the theory leaves room for preservation in alternative substrates that genuinely instantiate these properties -- perhaps biological tissue itself (cryopreservation), or engineered substrates that operate with continuous dynamics at multiple scales.

This makes biological computationalism specifically relevant to the vitrification work: if consciousness depends on the physical substrate's computational properties (not just its connectivity map), then preserving the actual biological tissue in a functional state is exactly the right approach.

### Open Questions

- Can the claims about scale-inseparability be formalized and tested?
- What specific experiment would distinguish biological computationalism from functionalism?
- If a neuromorphic chip with continuous dynamics and multiscale coupling were built, would it be conscious?
- How does this framework handle the fact that brains work fine with neurons replaced by prosthetics (cochlear implants, retinal implants)?
- Does anesthesia remove consciousness by disrupting scale-inseparability, or by something else entirely?

## Cross-Cutting Analysis

### The Landscape of Positions on Substrate Independence

The six papers define a spectrum:

1. **Full substrate independence (Butlin et al.):** Implement the right functional organization in any substrate and you get consciousness. Most optimistic for digital preservation.
2. **Constrained substrate independence (Aru & Milinkovic):** Substrate matters, but it's about computational TYPE (hybrid, scale-inseparable), not material. Moderately pessimistic for digital; optimistic for cryopreservation.
3. **Substrate-specific (IIT 4.0):** Consciousness requires intrinsic cause-effect power. Digital simulations lack this. Pessimistic for digital; neutral on biological preservation.
4. **Empirical uncertainty (COGITATE):** We don't even know which brain regions are necessary yet. Too early to say anything about preservation.

### The Unfalsifiability Problem

Every major theory of consciousness has an unfalsifiability problem at its core:
- **IIT:** Can't compute Phi for real systems; identity claim (consciousness = Phi) is metaphysical
- **GWT:** Can't test whether broadcast IS consciousness vs merely correlating with it
- **HOT:** Can't test whether higher-order representations constitute experience
- **Biological computationalism:** Can't test whether substrate properties are necessary vs incidental
- **Butlin framework:** Can't test whether satisfying indicators produces consciousness or just mimics it

The anti-IIT letter's "pseudoscience" charge applies to all of them equally. None of these theories can be straightforwardly falsified at their foundations. They can only be evaluated on their peripheral predictions, internal consistency, and explanatory power.

### What the Vitrification Results Mean for the Theoretical Debate

The vitrification paper is theory-agnostic but has different implications depending on which theory you accept:

- **Under IIT:** Preserved Phi-structure requires preserved causal architecture. If vitrification preserves the substrate's cause-effect structure (and the electrophysiology results suggest it partly does), it preserves the capacity for consciousness. But we can't compute Phi to verify.
- **Under GWT:** If the global workspace architecture survives vitrification, consciousness could resume. Preserved synaptic transmission and LTP are encouraging, but whole-brain workspace function was not tested.
- **Under biological computationalism:** Vitrification is the right approach because it preserves the actual biological substrate. The recovery of electrophysiology suggests the hybrid, scale-inseparable computation could resume. But the degraded short-term plasticity and altered excitability suggest it might not be fully intact.
- **Under Butlin's framework:** Irrelevant -- if consciousness is about functional organization, you just need to preserve the organization, whether in biological tissue or digital substrate.

### The State of the Field

Consciousness science in 2026 is in a position analogous to physics before the standard model: multiple competing theories, no consensus, limited empirical tools to discriminate between them, and foundational disagreements about what kind of thing consciousness even is. The COGITATE collaboration represents the best methodology available, but it tested only two theories on visual perception. The field needs:

1. More adversarial collaborations testing more theories
2. Non-visual consciousness studies (pain, emotion, proprioception)
3. Threshold/boundary experiments where theories diverge most
4. Formalization of theories that currently lack mathematical frameworks
5. Better proxy measures for quantities like Phi that are computationally intractable
6. Animal and clinical studies (anesthesia, disorders of consciousness) where ethical constraints allow

### Bottom Line

No one knows what consciousness is, how it arises, or whether it can be preserved through any known technology. The honest answer to every question about consciousness preservation is: "we don't know, and the current theories can't tell us with any confidence." What the papers DO establish is that (a) the empirical tools are improving, (b) theories can be partially tested and forced to evolve, (c) cryopreservation can preserve more neural function than previously thought, and (d) the substrate independence assumption underlying digital preservation is under serious theoretical challenge.
